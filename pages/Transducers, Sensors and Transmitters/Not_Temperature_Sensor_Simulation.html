Topics

Introduction to Thermistors Beginner

A thermistor is a type of resistor whose resistance changes significantly with temperature. The word comes from combining thermal and resistor. Unlike a standard resistor that changes only slightly, thermistors are designed specifically to exploit this temperature sensitivity.

There are two families:

  • NTC (Negative Temperature Coefficient) — resistance decreases as temperature rises. Made from metal oxide semiconductors. Most common type.
  • PTC (Positive Temperature Coefficient) — resistance increases as temperature rises. Ceramic (BaTiO₃) or polymer-based. Often used as a protection device.
Historical fact: The thermistor was invented by Samuel Ruben in 1930. Bell Labs commercialised it in the 1940s. Today, billions are produced annually.

Physical Construction

NTC thermistors are made by sintering metal oxide powders (manganese, cobalt, nickel, copper) at high temperature into a ceramic pellet. They are then formed into beads, discs, chips, or rods and protected with epoxy, glass, or stainless-steel housing.

Symbol and Marking

The standard IEC schematic symbol is a rectangle with a diagonal arrow and the letter "T" or "θ". The thermistor resistance is usually specified at 25°C (room temperature) — for example, "10kΩ NTC" means 10,000 Ω at exactly 25°C.

Key takeaway: Thermistors are the most sensitive and cost-effective electronic temperature sensors available. They outperform RTDs and thermocouples in the −50°C to +150°C range for sensitivity.

Semiconductor Physics Intermediate

Understanding why thermistors behave as they do requires some semiconductor physics. The key concept is charge carrier density.

For NTC Thermistors

Metal oxide thermistors behave like intrinsic semiconductors. At low temperatures, electrons are trapped in the valence band. As temperature rises, electrons gain thermal energy and cross the energy bandgap (Eg) into the conduction band — creating free electron-hole pairs that carry current.

n ∝ exp(−Eg / 2kT) Where: n = charge carrier concentration Eg = energy bandgap (eV) k = Boltzmann constant (8.617 × 10⁻⁵ eV/K) T = absolute temperature (Kelvin)

Because more carriers are available at higher temperatures, conductivity increases and resistance decreases. This is opposite to metals, where resistance increases due to phonon scattering.

For Ceramic PTC Thermistors (BaTiO₃)

Below the Curie temperature (Tc), BaTiO₃ is in a ferroelectric phase — the crystal lattice is slightly distorted, and electrons can tunnel through grain boundaries giving low resistance.

At Tc, the crystal undergoes a phase transition to a paraelectric phase. The grain boundary potential barriers increase dramatically, blocking electron flow. Resistance can jump by 6+ orders of magnitude in a narrow temperature range.

PTC Polymer type: Uses carbon black particles dispersed in a crystalline polymer. Below Tm (melting temperature), the polymer is crystalline and carbon particles form a conductive network. Above Tm, the polymer expands, separating the carbon particles and dramatically increasing resistance.

Conductivity Equation

σ = σ₀ × exp(−Ea / kT) Where: σ = electrical conductivity σ₀ = pre-exponential factor Ea = activation energy k = Boltzmann constant T = temperature (K) Since R ∝ 1/σ, resistance follows: R = R∞ × exp(+Ea / kT) [for NTC]

The Beta (β) Equation Intermediate

The most common simplified model for NTC thermistor resistance uses the Beta (β) parameter, also called the material constant or B-parameter.

R(T) = R₀ × exp[ β × (1/T − 1/T₀) ] Where: R(T) = Resistance at temperature T (Kelvin) R₀ = Resistance at reference temperature T₀ T₀ = Reference temperature (usually 298.15 K = 25°C) β = Beta constant (material-dependent, typically 2000–5000 K) T = Measurement temperature (Kelvin) Convert Celsius to Kelvin: T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15

Temperature Coefficient (α)

The Temperature Coefficient of Resistance (α) describes sensitivity — how much resistance changes per degree:

α = (1/R) × dR/dT = −β / T² Example: β = 3950 K, T = 298 K (25°C) α = −3950 / 298² = −0.0445 = −4.45% per °C This means for every 1°C rise, resistance drops ~4.45%
Limitation: The beta equation is accurate to only ±1–2°C over a wide range because β itself varies with temperature. It works well over narrow ranges (typically ±30°C from T₀). For wider ranges, use the Steinhart-Hart equation.

Worked Example

A 10kΩ NTC thermistor with β = 3950 K. What is its resistance at 50°C?

T₀ = 25°C = 298.15 K T = 50°C = 323.15 K R₀ = 10,000 Ω β = 3950 K R = 10000 × exp[3950 × (1/323.15 − 1/298.15)] R = 10000 × exp[3950 × (0.003094 − 0.003354)] R = 10000 × exp[3950 × (−0.000260)] R = 10000 × exp[−1.027] R = 10000 × 0.3584 R ≈ 3,584 Ω (3.584 kΩ)

Steinhart-Hart Model Advanced

For high-accuracy measurements over a wide temperature range, the Steinhart-Hart equation (1968) is the gold standard. It uses three empirically-determined coefficients to model the full R-T curve with ±0.01°C accuracy.

1/T = A + B·ln(R) + C·[ln(R)]³ Rearranged to find temperature from resistance: T(K) = 1 / { A + B·ln(R) + C·[ln(R)]³ } T(°C) = T(K) − 273.15 Typical coefficients for 10kΩ NTC at 25°C: A = 1.129148 × 10⁻³ B = 2.341077 × 10⁻⁴ C = 8.775468 × 10⁻⁸

Finding Coefficients

To determine A, B, C for a specific thermistor, measure resistance at three known temperatures (T₁, T₂, T₃) and solve the 3×3 linear system. Use temperatures spanning your operating range for best accuracy.

Using three calibration points (T₁,R₁), (T₂,R₂), (T₃,R₃): Let L₁ = ln(R₁), L₂ = ln(R₂), L₃ = ln(R₃) Solve the system: 1/T₁ = A + B·L₁ + C·L₁³ 1/T₂ = A + B·L₂ + C·L₂³ 1/T₃ = A + B·L₃ + C·L₃³
Accuracy comparison:
• Beta equation: ±0.5°C to ±2°C over wide range
• Steinhart-Hart: ±0.01°C to ±0.1°C over full range
• Use in: Medical devices, precision labs, industrial calibration

Extended Steinhart-Hart (4-coefficient)

1/T = A + B·ln(R) + C·[ln(R)]² + D·[ln(R)]³ The C·[ln(R)]² term improves accuracy further. Used in high-end process control instrumentation.

Voltage Divider Interface Intermediate

The most common circuit to read a thermistor with a microcontroller uses a voltage divider with a fixed resistor R₁ and the thermistor R_T. The output voltage V_out is fed into an ADC pin.

Circuit: V_supply → R₁ → V_out → R_T → GND V_out = V_supply × R_T / (R₁ + R_T) [thermistor to GND] or V_out = V_supply × R₁ / (R₁ + R_T) [thermistor to V+] For NTC (R_T ↓ as temp ↑): → With thermistor to GND: V_out decreases with temp → With thermistor to V+: V_out increases with temp For PTC (R_T ↑ as temp ↑): → Opposite of NTC for each configuration

Choosing Optimal R₁

To maximise sensitivity, R₁ should equal the thermistor resistance at the midpoint of your operating temperature range:

R₁_optimal = R_T(T_midpoint) Example: NTC 10kΩ, operating −10°C to +60°C Midpoint = 25°C → R_T ≈ 10kΩ → R₁ = 10kΩ ADC considerations: • ADC input impedance must be ≥10× R₁ • Use a buffer op-amp (unity gain) for low-Z ADC • 12-bit ADC resolution ≈ V_supply / 4096
Note on linearisation: The voltage divider output is still non-linear. Use a lookup table or Steinhart-Hart calculation in firmware to convert ADC counts → temperature accurately.

Self-Heating Effect Advanced

When current flows through a thermistor, power is dissipated as heat (I²R). This raises the thermistor's temperature above ambient — called self-heating — introducing measurement error.

ΔT_error = P_dissipated / δ Where: ΔT_error = Self-heating temperature error (°C) P = I² × R_therm (power in watts) δ = Dissipation constant (mW/°C, from datasheet) Typical: 1–10 mW/°C in still air Example: I = 1mA, R = 10kΩ, δ = 1.5 mW/°C P = (0.001)² × 10000 = 0.01 W = 10 mW ΔT = 10 / 1.5 = 6.7°C ← huge error! Better: I = 0.1mA (100µA): P = (0.0001)² × 10000 = 0.0001 W = 0.1 mW ΔT = 0.1 / 1.5 = 0.067°C ← acceptable

Minimisation Strategies

  • Use excitation current below 100µA for measurement-grade accuracy
  • Use pulsed excitation — power on only during ADC conversion
  • Select thermistors with high dissipation constant δ (good thermal contact)
  • Use oil bath or paste for immersion sensors
  • Allow settling time after powering circuit before reading ADC
Warning: NTC thermistors used for inrush current limiting deliberately exploit self-heating. As they heat up, resistance drops further — this is the intended behaviour for that application, not an error.

Linearisation Techniques Expert

The exponential R-T relationship of thermistors creates non-linearity that complicates analog signal processing. Several techniques are used to linearise the output.

Method 1: Parallel Resistor

Place resistor R_p in parallel with thermistor R_T: R_eff = (R_T × R_p) / (R_T + R_p) Best value: R_p = R_T at the inflection point of the curve This reduces sensitivity but gives more linear V-T response over a limited ±30°C range around the inflection point.

Method 2: Op-Amp Log Amplifier

An op-amp with a diode in the feedback path produces an output proportional to ln(R_T), partially linearising the response in analog hardware.

Method 3: Firmware Lookup Table (Most Common)

1. During design: compute R-T table at 1°C steps e.g., {-40°C: 341kΩ, -39°C: 317kΩ, ..., 150°C: 183Ω} 2. Store in microcontroller flash memory 3. At runtime: a. Read ADC → compute R_T b. Binary search lookup table c. Linear interpolation between two nearest points d. Output temperature in °C Memory: ~200 bytes for −40°C to +120°C at 1°C resolution Speed: <1µs execution on any modern MCU

Method 4: Steinhart-Hart in Firmware

float thermistorTemp(float resistance) { float ln_r = log(resistance); // natural log float inv_T = A + B * ln_r + C * ln_r * ln_r * ln_r; return (1.0 / inv_T) - 273.15; // Kelvin to Celsius } // Requires: float A, B, C constants from datasheet/calibration // Accuracy: ±0.01°C with correct coefficients
Best practice: For microcontroller systems, combine a good voltage divider (R₁ = R_T at midpoint), a 12-bit ADC, and Steinhart-Hart firmware calculation. This gives ±0.1°C over a wide range with no external analog processing.

Applications All Levels

NTC Thermistor Applications

  • Digital Thermometers — Oral/rectal/forehead thermometers: ±0.1°C accuracy. Fast response (1–3 s).
  • Battery Management Systems — Monitor Li-ion cell temperature during charging. Prevent thermal runaway in EVs and phones.
  • Inrush Current Limiting — NTC in series with power supply input. Cold = high R (limits inrush). Warm = low R (normal operation).
  • CPU/GPU Thermal Throttling — Embedded sensors trigger fan control and clock throttling to protect processors.
  • HVAC Systems — Room and duct temperature sensing in air conditioning and heating control loops.
  • Automotive ECU — Engine coolant, intake air, and exhaust temperature measurement for fuel injection optimisation.
  • Medical Devices — Catheter sensors, incubator control, dialysis machine monitoring, wearable health monitors.
  • Cold Chain Monitoring — Food and pharmaceutical storage temperature logging. HACCP compliance.
