Definition and Scope of Industrial Automation

Back to Course

Theory

Industrial automation is the use of technology, like robotics, control systems, and software, to operate machinery and processes in industries, replacing or reducing human intervention for tasks like monitoring, controlling, and decision-making, leading to increased efficiency, quality, safety, and productivity. It involves complex systems for managing flows of materials, information, and energy, integrating mechanical, electronic, and computer technologies.

Key Aspects

  • Goal: To perform tasks more effectively, reliably, and safely than manual methods, reducing errors and costs.
  • Technologies: Includes PLCs (Programmable Logic Controllers), sensors, robots, SCADA, machine vision, and computer-aided systems.
  • Scope: Extends beyond just manufacturing to areas like power grids, telecommunications, and logistics.
  • Function: Achieves detection, control, optimization, scheduling, and management of industrial operations.

Types of Industrial Automation Systems

1. Fixed Automation

Fixed automation often has a set task, continuous workflow, large volume production, and a high barrier of entry. Also called hard automation, this type of industrial automation rarely sees changes. The expense and time associated with a new product or changes in production are high. For lower volume or shorter product life cycles, programmable industrial automation will yield greater benefits.

2. Programmable Automation

Often associated with batch production, programmable automation works well for making several dozens to thousands of units. The lower production volume results in more changeovers which are considered when determining batch sizes and lead times. However, many companies are concerned with improving uptimes and increasing production. These goals are leading to more flexible automation.

3. Flexible Automation

As industrial automation types progress, the processes seem to involve less human interaction and downtime while increasing the range of products produced on one machine or production line. Flexible automation often involves precise electromechanical controls. A CNC machine is a good example. Changeovers can be performed automatically, reducing downtimes. This works well for batch production but allows for more in-demand production and increased customization.

4. Integrated Automation

Integrating devices and machines onto a single control system connects the dots between processes, production lines, and more. Integrated automation is a more holistic approach to manufacturing. As IIoT and Industry 4.0 advance, independent machines and production lines are able to communicate on a network to become increasingly flexible and move towards in-demand manufacturing and increased customization.

Scope of Industrial Automation

The scope of industrial automation is broad and keeps expanding as technology advances. It covers the following key areas:

1. Manufacturing Automation

  • Automated assembly lines
  • CNC machines and robotic arms
  • Packaging, labeling, and material handling
  • Automotive, electronics, textile, and consumer goods industries

2. Process Automation

  • Continuous processes like oil & gas, chemical plants, power generation
  • Batch processes in pharmaceuticals and food processing
  • Monitoring and control of temperature, pressure, flow, and level

3. Control Systems

  • PLC (Programmable Logic Controllers)
  • DCS (Distributed Control Systems)
  • SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition)
  • Sensors, actuators, and feedback devices

4. Robotics and Mechatronics

  • Industrial robots for welding, painting, and assembly
  • Collaborative robots (cobots) working alongside humans
  • Integration of mechanical, electrical, and control systems

5. Industrial Communication & Networking

  • Fieldbus systems (PROFIBUS, Modbus, CAN)
  • Industrial Ethernet
  • IoT and Industry 4.0 connectivity

6. Quality Control & Inspection

  • Machine vision systems
  • Automated testing and fault detection
  • Statistical process control (SPC)

7. Data, Analytics, and Smart Manufacturing

  • Manufacturing Execution Systems (MES)
  • Industrial IoT (IIoT)
  • Predictive maintenance and AI-driven optimization
