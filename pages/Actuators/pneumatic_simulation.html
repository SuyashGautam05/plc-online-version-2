Pneumatic Actuators

A pneumatic actuator converts compressed air energy into mechanical motion — either linear (back-and-forth) or rotary. In automated industrial systems, they are controlled by a PLC (Programmable Logic Controller) via solenoid valves and ladder logic programs.

Industrial Context: Pneumatic actuators are found in assembly lines, packaging machines, robotic arms, conveyor systems, and material handling equipment. They are preferred for their speed, safety in explosive environments, and low maintenance.

How Compressed Air Creates Motion

When compressed air (typically 4–8 bar) enters a cylinder, it pushes against a piston. The piston is connected to a rod that extends outward. When air is vented, a spring or opposing air pressure returns the piston. This simple principle drives thousands of industrial applications.

The force generated depends on the air pressure and piston bore area: F = P × A, where F is force (N), P is pressure (Pa), and A is area (m²).

Types of Pneumatic Cylinders

1. Single-Acting Cylinder (SAC): Air pressure acts on one side only. A return spring moves the piston back when air is exhausted. Simpler, uses one air port. Used where force is only needed in one direction (clamping, lifting).

2. Double-Acting Cylinder (DAC): Air pressure acts alternately on both sides. Requires a 5/2 or 4/2 way valve. Provides controlled force in both extend and retract directions — most common in industrial automation.

3. Rodless Cylinder: The piston moves inside a sealed tube, carrying a carriage on the outside via a magnetic coupling or band seal. Used for long strokes where a conventional rod would be too long.

4. Rotary Actuator: Converts linear piston motion to rotation via rack-and-pinion or vane mechanism. Provides 90°, 180°, or 270° of rotation for indexing and turning applications.

Safety Note: Pneumatic systems store significant energy. Always depressurize before maintenance. Install exhaust silencers to reduce noise. Use lockout/tagout procedures.

PLC Control & Solenoid Valves

A PLC outputs a 24VDC signal to a solenoid coil. This coil generates a magnetic field that shifts a spool inside the valve, re-routing compressed air to the appropriate cylinder port. The PLC program (ladder logic) defines when and how long to energize the solenoid.

The 3/2 way valve has 3 ports (pressure, cylinder, exhaust) and 2 positions. It controls single-acting cylinders. The 5/2 way valve has 5 ports and 2 positions, controlling both ports of a double-acting cylinder.

Ladder Logic Programming

Ladder Logic (LD) is the most common PLC programming language. It graphically resembles relay logic diagrams. Each rung represents a control logic equation. Contacts (inputs) and coils (outputs) are connected by horizontal power rails.

A simple extend sequence: Start button (I0.0) closes → Solenoid Y1 (Q0.0) energizes → Cylinder extends → Limit switch LS1 (I0.1) closes → Timer T1 runs → Retract solenoid Y2 (Q0.1) energizes.

Formulas

Extend Force
F = P × (π × d²/4)
P = pressure (Pa), d = bore dia (m)
Retract Force (DAC)
F = P × (π/4)(d²− dr²)
dr = rod diameter (m)
Air Consumption
Q = A × S × N × R
A=area, S=stroke, N=cycles/min, R=compression ratio
