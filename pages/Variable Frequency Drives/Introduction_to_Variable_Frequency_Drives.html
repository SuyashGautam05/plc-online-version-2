Introduction to Variable Frequency Drives

A variable frequency drive (VFD) is a device that controls the speed and torque of an AC motor by adjusting the frequency and voltage of the power supply. A VFD can also regulate the acceleration and deceleration of the motor during start-up and stop, respectively. A VFD consists of three main components: a rectifier, an inverter, and a control system.

A VFD is used for various purposes, such as:

  • Saving energy and improving system efficiency
  • Converting power in hybridization applications
  • Matching the speed or power of the motor to the process requirements
  • Improving the working environment by lowering noise and vibration
  • Reducing mechanical stress on the motor and extending its lifetime
  • Shaving peak consumption and reducing the motor size required

VFD is commonly used in applications that involve fans, pumps, compressors, conveyors, crushers, mills, lifts, escalators, etc. A VFD can provide significant benefits over other types of motor controllers, such as soft starters or across-the-line contactors.

Five Main Types of Variable Frequency Drives (VFDs)

1. Voltage Source Inverter (VSI) Drives

VSI drives use a DC voltage source obtained from a rectifier and DC-link capacitors. The inverter converts this fixed DC voltage into variable-frequency and variable-voltage AC output.

Applications:
Fans, pumps, compressors, conveyors

Advantages:

  • Simple structure
  • High efficiency
  • Widely available and cost-effective

2. Current Source Inverter (CSI) Drives

CSI drives use a DC current source, maintained by large inductors. The inverter regulates output current rather than voltage.

Applications:
Medium- and high-power drives, synchronous motors, regenerative systems

Advantages:

  • Inherent short-circuit protection
  • High reliability
  • Suitable for regenerative operation

3. Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) Drives

PWM drives control motor voltage and frequency by varying the width of inverter switching pulses. This results in smooth motor operation and low harmonic distortion.

Applications:
General-purpose industrial motors, HVAC systems, machine tools

Advantages:

  • Good speed control
  • Reduced harmonic distortion
  • High energy efficiency

4. Direct Torque Control (DTC) Drives

DTC drives directly control motor torque and stator flux without using PWM or an encoder. Switching decisions are made based on instantaneous torque and flux values.

Applications:
Cranes, hoists, elevators, extruders

Advantages:

  • Very fast torque response
  • High torque accuracy
  • No encoder required

5. Multi-Level Inverter Drives

These drives generate output voltage using multiple voltage levels instead of a single high-voltage step, reducing stress on power devices and improving output waveform quality.

Applications:
Medium- and high-voltage drives, large pumps, compressors, power plants

Advantages:

  • Lower harmonic distortion
  • Reduced dv/dt stress
  • High efficiency at high power levels
